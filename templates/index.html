How the movement-matchup overlays are made

Video requirements

  • A front-facing, mostly stationary camera.
  • A 1280×720-like framing with the pitcher in approximately the same region.
  • A clearly visible pitcher wearing relatively bright clothing against a darker background.
  • The ball becoming visible shortly after release.
  • Similar delivery geometry across pitches.
  • One pitcher and one continuous camera setup per video.

What the tool does

The video is split into individual deliveries using the existing pose-based method. A baseball detector calculates a ballpath for each accepted pitch. Paths are grouped by relative flight time, horizontal and vertical movement, and late departure, then assigned best-fit pitch-type labels.

How synchronization works

Each motion-detected delivery is searched only within its likely pre-peak release window. The pitcher’s pose is compared with one common session reference for coarse synchronization. A second ball-track pass then corrects the release timing by at most five frames, accounting for pitches that release slightly earlier or later relative to the same body pose. The background is registered to reduce small camera shifts, and the two synchronized source clips are blended. A freeze is baked into the video at the approximately 150-millisecond hitter decision point; it lasts one second at real-time playback and four seconds in slow-motion mode.

What the tool does not do

For each cross-type matchup, every eligible path pair receives a tunneling score out of 100. Decision-point proximity is weighted more heavily than separation after the decision point, though both are required for a strong score. Only the single strongest pair for that pitch-type matchup is rendered. The baseballs are tinted with the same group colors used in the movement chart; no tracer is added. Pitch labels remain video-based inferences, not verified pitch calls, because a front-view camera cannot observe grip, spin axis, or intent.
